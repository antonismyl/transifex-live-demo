Designing for clarity: what we learned redesigning our dashboard.

Sofia Romano · Jan 30, 2026 · 5 min read

The Lumen dashboard has had the same shape since our first release. It served us well. It also picked up barnacles — widgets, menus, little toggles added in a hurry and never removed. Last quarter we rebuilt it. Here's what changed, and what we tried and abandoned.

The problem wasn't the charts

Going in, we assumed the rewrite would be about charts — better types, faster rendering, more configurability. We were wrong. The charts were fine. The problem was everything around the charts: filters, time ranges, comparisons, saved views. Users told us they spent more time searching for a saved report than reading the report itself.

A hierarchy of decisions

When someone opens a dashboard, they're making a sequence of decisions. We listed every decision out loud, in order:

  1. "Am I looking at the right thing?"
  2. "Is the time range right?"
  3. "What am I comparing it against?"
  4. "Are there any filters applied I should know about?"
  5. "What's the chart telling me?"

Then we made the UI answer them in that order, top-to-bottom. Dashboard name and audience at the top. Time range just below. Comparison and filters in a single row that's visible without scrolling. The chart itself gets the whole bottom half of the screen.

A good design answers the user's next question before they think to ask it.

Things we tried and removed

A surprising amount of the redesign was removing things we'd already built:

  • A "power mode" that showed every filter at once. It looked impressive. Nobody used it.
  • Inline annotations on charts. Good idea, wrong venue — we moved them to a side panel.
  • A floating action menu that followed the cursor. Felt magical in the prototype. Felt nauseating in use.

Type and density

The biggest visual change is density. The old dashboard was a 12px world — lots of labels, lots of numbers, lots of chrome. The new one is closer to 14–15px everywhere, with letter-spacing: -0.02em on the larger headings so they don't look floaty. Fewer elements, each a little bigger, each easier to read.

We also made peace with white space. The old dashboard was 80% information, 20% chrome, 0% breathing room. The new one leaves a little air around every group — not enough to feel empty, but enough that the eye has somewhere to rest.

What's next

We're not done. There's a long tail of smaller pieces — exports, share sheets, embedded views — that we'll rework over the next quarter. And we're still learning from how people use the new dashboard. If something feels off, tell us. We're listening.
